What are Mansfield's distinctive sale challenges?

A meaningful share of Mansfield stock is harder to sell through an estate agent than the headline market data suggests, because mortgage lenders refuse to lend on it without conditions and many buyers walk away rather than navigate them. We end up buying a lot of Mansfield properties that the open market struggles with, for five specific reasons.

The Coal Authority CON29M and the nine-named-colliery map

Practically the entire Mansfield District sits within the Development High Risk Area for the Nottinghamshire coalfield. The Mining Remediation Authority (the public body renamed from the Coal Authority on 28 November 2024) maintains the National Coal Mining Database at its NG18 4RG headquarters and issues the CON29M Residential mining search that every Mansfield-area conveyance requires. The 2026 search costs £35.10 inclusive of VAT and turns around in 5-10 working days. Nine former collieries define the local Coal Authority map: Mansfield Colliery / Crown Farm (closed March 1988), Pleasley Colliery (closed 1983), Sherwood Colliery (closed 1992), Shirebrook Colliery (closed 1993), Welbeck Colliery (closed May 2010), Thoresby Colliery (closed July 2015: the last working pit in Nottinghamshire), Clipstone Colliery (closed 2003), Bilsthorpe Colliery (closed 1997), and Rufford / Ollerton Collieries (closed 1993 and 1994). Across NG19 Forest Town, NG20 Pleasley / Shirebrook and NG21 Bilsthorpe / Clipstone, a CON29M flag for shallow workings within 30 metres of surface, a recorded subsidence claim, or a mine entry within 20 metres is routine rather than exceptional. Retail mortgage lenders increasingly demand a Coal Mining Risk Assessment or specialist mining-aware structural survey before lending against flagged stock. Cash buyers do not need a mortgage approval and so a CON29M flag does not block the purchase, it is the structural reason cash sale is disproportionately useful across the Mansfield ex-coalfield belt. The Coal Authority responds to 800+ subsidence claims and incidents a year across the British coalfield as a whole.

Mine gas (methane and stythe) and contaminated land

Abandoned mines continue to release methane and carbon dioxide ("stythe") into overlying ground; the risk concentrates on shallow workings under or near residential property. Planning authorities including Mansfield District Council now routinely require ground gas risk assessments for new build on former colliery sites, and surveyors will sometimes flag a mine-gas risk that requires further investigation before a buyer can secure mortgage approval. Separately, MDC has adopted a Contaminated Land Strategy under Part 2A of the Environmental Protection Act 1990 covering identification and remediation of historically contaminated sites: colliery spoil tips, former gasworks, foundry and brickworks footprints, and the long industrial corridor along the River Maun. Planning conditions on brownfield land typically require Phase 1 desktop and Phase 2 intrusive investigation before development, and Environmental Search results at conveyancing will flag contaminated-land proximity that some lenders treat as adverse.

PRC defective stock and the MDC non-traditional construction register

Mansfield District Council's Freedom of Information disclosure register confirms it holds a Non-Traditional Construction housing-stock list covering its own retained council stock, with Wates, Cornish, Airey, Reema, Hawksley, Orlit, BISF and Trusteel types all represented in pockets, primarily on the post-war estate clearance sites: Bull Farm, Pleasley Hill, Ladybrook, Manor estate (Mansfield Woodhouse), Bellamy estate and Oak Tree Lane. Right-to-Buy sales over forty years have pushed many of these into private hands. Several of these Pre-cast Reinforced Concrete (PRC) types were classified as "defective" under the Housing Defects Act 1984: Airey, Reema, Wates, Cornish, Orlit, Unity, Hawksley. Most high-street mortgage lenders will not lend on these without a full PRC structural repair scheme and a PRC Certificate from a qualified PRC structural engineer. A seller of a defective-PRC house in NG18 or NG19 faces a structurally cash-only market: this is the single largest predictor of long agency exposure in the district. We buy them.

Flood risk, the River Maun, Bleak Hills and The Park

The Environment Agency's Flood Warning Area "River Maun at Mansfield" covers Bleak Hills and The Park ward. Flood Re, the joint government-insurance industry reinsurance scheme that caps annual flood premiums for at-risk homes, applies to dwellings built on or before 1 January 2009 and excludes residential buildings built after that date. Properties on the Bleak Hills / The Park / lower River Maun corridor that fall into Flood Zone 2 or 3 face annual flood-insurance premium variation; post-2009 builds are excluded from Flood Re entirely. Retail buyers often walk away on insurance grounds. We complete on flood-zone Mansfield stock.

Tenant-in-situ exit and the Renters' Rights Act 2025

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 commenced on 1 May 2026, abolishing Section 21 "no-fault" evictions. To regain possession to sell, landlords now use Ground 1A: minimum 12-month tenancy first, four months' notice, plus a 12-month re-let ban if circumstances change and they decide to keep the property after all. End-to-end that is a six-to-eight-month process, plus the cost of compliance. A cash sale with the tenant in situ avoids Ground 1A entirely, the new owner takes the tenancy across as part of the purchase. The Renters' Rights Act has driven a measurable accelerated exit signal across NG18 ex-council stock, NG19 1960s semi BTL, NG20 Shirebrook warehouse-economy lets and NG21 ex-colliery BTL.
