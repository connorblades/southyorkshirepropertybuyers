What are Rotherham's distinctive sale challenges?

A meaningful share of Rotherham stock is harder to sell on the open market than the headline data suggests, because mortgage lenders won't lend on it without conditions and many buyers walk away rather than navigate those conditions. We end up buying a lot of Rotherham properties the open market struggles with — for six specific reasons.

Coal Authority high-risk across S62, S63, S64 and S66

Almost the entire eastern half of the borough sits inside a Coal Mining Reporting Area. The Coal Authority was renamed the Mining Remediation Authority on 1 July 2024. Eight of South Yorkshire's twelve historic deep mines lay within or adjacent to Rotherham — Cortonwood, Manvers Main, Wath Main, Kilnhurst, Silverwood, Roundwood, Thrybergh Hall and Maltby Main. The CON29M Mining Report is c.£35 inc VAT and 5–10 working days. Where it flags shallow workings less than 30 metres — common across S62, S63, S64 and S66 — lenders typically require a specialist mining engineer's report (£600–£2,500), an indemnity policy (£75–£300), or reduce the LTV offer. A cash buyer doesn't need a mortgage approval, so the mining position doesn't block the purchase.

PRC and defective pre-cast concrete housing

The Housing Defects Act 1984 — consolidated into Part XVI of the Housing Act 1985 — formally designates certain pre-cast reinforced concrete (PRC) house types as defective. Rotherham types include Cornish Unit, Tarran, Airey, Reema Hollow Panel, Wimpey No-Fines, Wates, Unity, Boot, Smith and Whitson-Fairhurst — concentrated in pockets of S61 Kimberworth Park, S63 Bolton-upon-Dearne and S66 Maltby. Without a PRC repair certificate (the Code 6 / PRC Homes Limited certificate), mainstream mortgage lenders won't lend. Open-market sales of unmodified PRC stock routinely collapse at the lender's surveyor's report. We buy these without the certificate.

Selective Licensing — seven designated wards from 15 February 2026

Rotherham MBC's Selective Licensing Scheme commenced 15 February 2026 and runs until 14 February 2031, covering seven designated areas: Brinsworth, Dinnington, Rotherham town centre and Eastwood, Masbrough and Kimberworth, Parkgate, and Thurcroft. The fee is £995 per property for the 5-year term (£210 admin + £785 maintenance), with discounts for portfolios and previous-scheme rebate holders. Operating an unlicensed property in a designated area is an offence under Housing Act 2004 Part 3 carrying a civil penalty up to £30,000 or unlimited fine on prosecution. Approximately 4,132 properties are in scope. Notably, Brinsworth — directly affected by Liberty Steel — is on the list. A tenant-in-situ cash sale transfers the licensing question to the new owner.

Empty-homes 100% premium — activated 1 April 2026

Rotherham is the last of the four South Yorkshire boroughs to activate the doubled empty-homes premium under the Levelling Up and Regeneration Act 2023 (Sheffield, Doncaster and Barnsley triggered theirs in April 2024). From 1 April 2026 Rotherham charges a 100% premium on dwellings empty for 12 months or more, and on second homes. For an inherited Band D Rotherham property held empty after that date the annual council tax doubles — adding roughly £2,300–£2,500 per year before any other costs. Probate executors who were comfortable holding it for now suddenly are not.

Renters' Rights Act 2025 — Ground 1A and the 12-month re-let ban

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 commenced on 1 May 2026, abolishing Section 21 "no-fault" evictions. To regain vacant possession for sale, landlords now use Ground 1A — four months' notice, plus a 12-month re-let ban if the property doesn't actually sell. For a Rotherham landlord that's potentially a year of carrying costs without rental income. NRLA Q4 2025 data found 41% of small landlords (1–4 properties) plan to sell at least one home within twelve months — concentrated in low-yield S63/S64 BTL stock. A cash sale with the tenant in situ avoids the Ground 1A notice and re-let ban entirely — the new owner takes the AST across at completion under Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 s.3.

Japanese knotweed and TA6 disclosure after Patarkatsishvili v Woodward-Fisher

The Don corridor (Templeborough, Brinsworth, Aldwarke, Kilnhurst, Mexborough) and Dearne corridor (Wath through Mexborough) have been documented Japanese knotweed colonisation corridors since the 1980s. Properties within c.200m of either main channel routinely attract surveyor flagging. Patarkatsishvili v Woodward-Fisher [2025] EWHC 265 (Ch) makes non-disclosure on the TA6 Property Information Form demonstrably actionable in misrepresentation — including for damp, infestation, knotweed, mining and prior insurance claims. We buy regardless; the seller still warrants what they know, but the cash route doesn't depend on a clean knotweed certificate.
