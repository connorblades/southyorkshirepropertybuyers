**Target:** [property118.com](https://www.property118.com/) — UK landlord community, ~150K monthly readers, accepts member contributions
**Why a fit:** their audience is exactly who buys SYPB's Tier 3 services — landlords thinking about exiting
**Backlink value:** dofollow author bio link, member profile page, links from any article comments

### Pitch email

> Working title: "Three realistic ways to sell a tenanted property in 2026 — and the maths behind each"
>
> The Renters' Rights Act has changed the maths for landlords looking to exit. With Ground 1A's four-month notice, the 12-month re-let ban, and fines of up to £40k for procedural mistakes, advice that still references Section 21 is leaving readers exposed.
>
> The piece walks through evict-then-sell, BTL-investor open-market sale, and cash-buyer-with-tenant-in-situ — with realistic timelines, price ranges, and net-proceeds maths for each. ~1,400 words, original to Property118, ready in 5 working days.
>
> Author bio includes my own site (clearly disclosed quick-sale operator).

### Headline options

1. Three realistic ways to sell a tenanted property in 2026 — and the maths behind each
2. Selling a tenanted property post-Renters' Rights Act: evict, list, or sell-with-tenant
3. The buy-to-let exit playbook for 2026 — three routes compared

### Outline

1. Why selling a tenanted property is harder in 2026 (Ground 1A vs Section 21, 4-month notice, 12-month re-let ban, fines £7k–£40k)
2. **Option A: Evict, then sell vacant.** Timeline 9–14 months. Cost: 6–9 months of rent loss + court fees. Best price (10–15% premium for vacant possession). When this works.
3. **Option B: Sell to a BTL investor on the open market.** Timeline 4–7 months. Tenancy transfers; tenant stays. Buyer pool 70–80% smaller; price 10–20% below VP. When this works.
4. **Option C: Sell to a cash buyer with tenant in situ.** Timeline 2–4 weeks. 80–85% of VP. Speed/certainty trade-off. When this works.
5. **Net-proceeds maths.** Worked example on a £180k VP-value property — net to seller across all three routes. Often closer than headlines suggest.
6. The non-financial factors. Tenant relationship, time, court risk, "tired landlord" reality.
